  LSD and psilocybin work mainly through the 5-HT2A receptor, which increases

  the same time. Psilocybin is shorter and a bit "cleaner" than LSD, which may
  be why people often describe it as slightly more settling. Both quiet the
  brain's default-mode network (the self-referential, ruminating hub), and both
  have a well-documented afterglow. Clinical evidence for depression is growing
  but still rests on small trials that are hard to blind.

The classic psychedelics can make the world vivid and loosen the grip of rigid,
ruminating thought — which is part of why rigid low moods sometimes lift after
them. But they're activating, not calming, in the body: heart rate goes up, and
the experience can be as edgy as it is expansive.

Held honestly, these sit in the "borrowed state" column, and firmly in the
"only with real support" column. The afterglow and the openness they can leave
behind are genuine, but they become lasting change through integration and
practice, not through the trip alone. As everywhere on this page: no dosing, no
sourcing, no how-to — the point here is understanding what these states are and
aren't, and remembering the room they hint at is reachable by safer doors.
