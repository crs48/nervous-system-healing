Most of what people mean by "working with your nervous system" starts with
being able to tell, roughly, which state you're in. Here's a fuller version of
the tour from the [Start Here path](/nervous-system-healing/start/2-meet-the-states/).

## Settled (safe enough)

The state where you can think clearly, feel your feelings without being
swamped, connect with people, and rest. Things don't have to be perfect —
there's just enough room. When people talk about a "window of tolerance," this
is the inside of the window: the zone where you can handle what's happening
without tipping over into panic or shutting down.

## Activated (fight-or-flight)

Energy up and mobilized. Anxious, urgent, irritable, restless, scanning for
what's wrong. The body is getting ready to deal with a threat — heart faster,
muscles readier, attention narrowed. Useful in a genuine emergency; miserable
when it won't switch off after the emergency is over.

## Shut down

The opposite direction. When fighting or fleeing isn't possible or didn't
work, the system can pull the plug to protect you: flat, numb, foggy, heavy,
far away. It's often misread as laziness or depression, but underneath it's a
survival setting — the body conserving itself when it can't win.

## The blends — dials, not switches

This is the part that's easy to miss and worth really taking in: these aren't
three separate boxes you're locked into. They're more like dials, and more
than one can be turned up at once.

The most common blend people recognize themselves in is **"wired and tired"** —
activated underneath (anxious, can't settle) *and* shut down on top
(exhausted, flat, foggy) at the same time. Foot on the gas and the brake
together. If that's the one that lands for you, you're not broken and you're
very much not alone — it's one of the most common places a stressed nervous
system gets stuck.

## A note on the map

We talk about these states because the language is genuinely useful — it
helps people locate themselves and be kinder about what they find. It's worth
knowing that the *specific science* behind the popular version of this model
(polyvagal theory) is debated. We use it as a helpful **map**. The map isn't
the territory — but a good map still helps you find your way. There's more on
that honesty point in the [modality entry for polyvagal-informed
work](/nervous-system-healing/modalities/polyvagal-informed/).
