If there's one idea worth carrying out of this whole site, it's this one:
healing a nervous system is slow, small, regular work — and that's not a
limitation to push past. It's how it works.

## What "capacity" means

Think of the range of intensity you can handle while still staying yourself —
still able to think, feel, and stay present. That range is your **window of
tolerance**, and "capacity" is basically how wide it is. Inside the window,
you can meet what's happening. Push past the top and you flood (panic, rage,
overwhelm); drop past the bottom and you shut down (numbness, collapse). In
either place, the thinking part of the brain goes offline and nothing really
gets integrated.

A hard past tends to *narrow* the window. A lot of healing is simply widening
it again — a bit at a time.

## Why slow, and why pushing backfires

The rest-and-repair side of the nervous system — the part you actually need
for healing — only comes online when there's a felt sense of safety and
no pressure. Which sets up a genuine paradox: **trying hard to force calm is
itself a kind of pressure**, a low-grade "not safe yet" signal. You can't
white-knuckle your way into feeling safe. This is why "just relax" never
worked, and why treating healing as a productivity project tends to make a
driven person more dysregulated, not less.

So the pace isn't a compromise. Going slower, in small doses, with room to
rest, is the actual mechanism. In this world people say "less is more, and
slower is better," and "the slower you go, the faster you get there." With a
nervous system, slower usually *is* the fast way.

## Titration — a drop at a time

The word practitioners use for this is **titration**, borrowed from
chemistry: if you add a strong reagent all at once you get an explosion, but
add it one drop at a time and the reaction stays manageable. Applied to
healing, it means approaching hard material in tiny amounts, touching an edge
for a few seconds and then coming back to something steady — never flooding
the system. That "touch and return" is also how the window gradually widens.

## Healing isn't a straight line

Finally: progress here is not a staircase. It's more like a spiral. You'll
circle back to things you thought you'd handled — but from a little higher up
each time, with a bit more room to meet them. A hard week after a good stretch
isn't proof you've gone backwards; sometimes it means your system finally
trusts you enough to let something surface. Judging your progress by a single
day or week is one of the quickest ways to lose heart. Zoom out to months.

There's no rush. You have the rest of your life to be gentle with yourself.
